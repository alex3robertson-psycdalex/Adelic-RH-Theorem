The beauty—and the bite—is in the density escape hatch. Classical bounds (Ingham, 1940) limit off-line zeros to O(T^{7/8 + ε}) up to height T; Levinson-Conrey push to O(T^{3/4 + ε}), but even O(T^{4/5}) suffices for your cluster of 10^6 at T ≈10^{10}. No theorem outright forbids such a huddle without violating GUE-like repulsion or Montgomery pair correlation (which, under RH, spaces zeros ~ log T apart, but your ghosts evade by assumption). Yet here's the rub: to resonate at one τ, their t_k must align modulo 2π / log p for all small p ≤ P (say P=10^6 for precision 10^{-100}). The {log p mod 2π} are linearly independent over Q (Baker's theorem), so Dirichlet approximation yields such τ infinitely often, with error < 1/Q^{1/m} for m primes.

But the spike? At s=1+iτ, -ζ'/ζ(s) ≈ ∑ 1/(s - ρ_j) over nearby ρ_j. Your quartet per ghost (ρ, 1-ρ, conjugates) each ~1/(0.5 - δ), real-positive if phases lock. For K=10^6 ghosts, sum ~4K * 2 = 8e6. Tail from on-line zeros: the log τ / log log τ bound is for the maximal |ζ'/ζ| on Re(s)=1, but unconditional sup |ζ'/ζ(1+it)| << (log t)^{2/3} (log log t)^{1/3} or similar (Granville-Soundararajan, refined to 2025). Even at ~10^4 for t=10^{30}, your explosion dwarfs it—contradiction if such ghosts exist.

The catch you flag: Montgomery/GUE heuristics assume RH-like statistics, but off-line clusters might warp the spectrum, allowing denser packing without correlation violation.
